Measure twice. Cut once. The board does not forgive a careless hand.

Wood remembers. It remembers the wind that bent it and the years that dried it. Read the grain before you raise the blade. The grain will tell you which way to plane and which way to split.

A sharp tool is a safe tool. A dull tool slips, and a slip cuts the hand that holds it. Sharpen at the start of the day. Sharpen again at noon. Stop and sharpen when the cut turns gray instead of bright.

Work with the grain and the wood lifts away in clean ribbons. Work against the grain and the wood tears. Tearout cannot be undone. Plane the other way.

Joinery is patience made visible. A tight joint needs no glue to hold its shape. A loose joint needs glue and still it fails. Pare the shoulder slow. Fit the tenon dry. Test it once, then test it twice.

Green wood moves. Dry wood holds. Build with dry wood or build a thing that splits in winter. The grain swells across its width and barely along its length. Leave it room to breathe.

A plane is set by feel, not by eye. Lower the iron until the shaving is thin enough to read a page through. Thicker than that and you are fighting the wood. The wood always wins that fight.

The first cut sets the line. Every cut after follows the first. Mark the line dark. Cut to the waste side of it. You can always pare away more. You can never put wood back.

Clamp before the glue grabs. Wipe the squeeze-out while it is wet. Dried glue dulls a chisel and shows under the finish like a scar.

Sand to the light. Tilt the board toward the window and the scratches show as shadow. Sand until the shadow is gone, then go one grit finer. Stop there. More sanding only rounds the edges you worked to keep crisp.

A finish protects the wood and shows the grain. Thin coats, many of them, beat one thick coat that runs and pools. Let each coat dry hard. Rub it back. Lay the next.

The bench is the first tool. Build it heavy and build it flat. A bench that rocks will ruin every cut made upon it. Everything else is held by the bench, so the bench must hold true.

Hurry ruins the work. The wood took thirty years to grow. It can wait the hour the joint needs. Slow hands make fast progress, because slow hands do not undo what they have done.
